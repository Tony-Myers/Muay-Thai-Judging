Scoring hierarchy

Muay Thai scoring is top-down

Judges work from the most decisive factors down to the least decisive. If Level 1 is clear, lower levels rarely overturn the decision.

Direction is neutral: a boxer can score moving forward, backward, sideways, or on the ropes. Advancing only matters when it creates effect or control.

How to apply the hierarchy in real time

  1. Start with effect and dominance: who is doing the more effective work in Muay Thai terms?
  2. Use balance and posture as a major separator: who stays composed; who is broken, turned, stumbled, or forced to reset?
  3. Then consider clean scoring weapons on targets: body kicks, knees and teep often signal stronger scoring when delivered with control.
  4. Then control of space and clinch: not “walking forward”, but making the opponent give ground or lose position unwillingly.
  5. Finally, defence and counters: defence that leads immediately to a clean, balanced scoring return.

Strong vs weak signals

Strong signals (often decisive)
  • Displacement or posture break.
  • Clear visible hurt or change in behaviour.
  • Clean body kick/knee/teep delivered on balance and not convincingly returned.
Weak signals (only matter if otherwise close)
  • High activity with little effect.
  • Pressure that is neutralised or easily evaded.
  • Contact absorbed/checked with posture unaffected.
Common misconception: “forward pressure wins”
Forward pressure is not automatically scoring. What matters is whether the pressure produces effect, disrupts balance, or demonstrates control. A counter fighter moving backward can win if they remain in control and score cleanly.
