What scores

What scores (and what usually does not)

All legal techniques can score. In practice, actions score higher when they create clearer effect, show balance and control, and disrupt the opponent’s posture or behaviour.

High-value actions (when clean and balanced)

  • Body kicks (especially to ribs/torso) that visibly move the opponent or are not checked well.
  • Knees (especially to body) with clear effect.
  • Teep when it off-balances, disrupts rhythm, or demonstrates control of range.
  • Clinch dominance: turning, off-balancing, posture breaking, clean knees.
  • Dumps/sweeps when executed cleanly with the scorer remaining balanced (timing and control matter).

Lower-value or conditional actions

  • Punches: can score, but usually need unmistakable effect (stun, knockdown, visible damage, sustained dominance) to outweigh kicks and knees.
  • Low kicks: score more when they clearly compromise stance or mobility.
  • Busy combinations: low scoring if blocked/absorbed, delivered off-balance, or immediately countered.
Rule of thumb: “Touching is not scoring; effect is scoring.”

Targets, effect, and “quality of delivery”

Judges cannot feel impact. They infer effect using visual cues, especially: (a) whether the technique lands cleanly on target, (b) the balance and posture of both boxers, (c) sharpness and return to stance, and (d) whether the opponent’s position or behaviour changes.

  • Effect: disturbing the opponent’s physical equilibrium (loss of position) or psychological equilibrium (visible pain, hesitation or fear).
  • Sharp techniques: begin balanced, delivered sharply on balance, and return to a good balanced position.
  • Target vs effect: some targets can score even without big visual effect (clean body/neck; across the back), particularly when delivered with clear balance and control.

Clinch scoring

What counts
  • Control of position and turning.
  • Posture breaking (pulling down, off-balancing).
  • Knees landing clean on scoring targets.
  • Opponent neutralised and unable to answer.
What does not count much
  • Holding without effect.
  • Smothered or weak knees.
  • Mutual stalling where neither gains clear control.

Direction is not scoring

Counter fighters can win moving backward or sideways if they move in a controlled way, maintain position, and score. Attack fighters can win by advancing if they maintain position and score.
